## 0. Executive Summary

Post-training alignment changes how a language model behaves. After alignment via any fine-tuning method (SFT, RLHF, DPO, RLVR/GRPO, instruction-tuning, and the like), a model refuses some requests it used to answer, replaces specific indigenous terms with generic Western ones, and sometimes appears to lose facts it demonstrably had before alignment. The improvement is selective: the same training that makes the model more helpful on average degrades it on specific kinds of culturally specific content. This selectivity is documented behaviorally but its mechanism inside the network is not understood.

This project asks three nested questions. First, when alignment changes behavior on culturally specific content, does it rewrite the model's internal representations of that content, or does it leave the representations intact and install a late-stage policy that redirects the output? Second, is the answer the same for every type of cultural content, or do different categories (regional facts, indigenous vocabulary, sensitive topics) get treated by different mechanisms? Third, to the extent we can tell from available models, does the effect depend on who performed the alignment and how, or is it intrinsic to the post-training objective?

We answer these questions by building a 6,000-item controlled probe set of Indian cultural knowledge (ICCD-6K), running it through paired model checkpoints (base models and their post-trained versions), and using mechanistic interpretability tools to locate, classify, and causally test the representational changes. Each item is a minimal pair of sentence prefixes differing in one cultural anchor plus a token-level target; the per-item readout is a log-odds difference (delta-L, in nats), never a raw probability, for the reason given in Section 2. Indian culture is both the controlled testbed for this mechanistic question and a genuine subject the study cares about: it offers rich documented content, an empirically validated regional structure, and the availability of an Indic-focused post-trained model whose comparison to other aligned models is informative.

The probe set sits in the CounterFact/ROME lineage (Meng et al., NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2202.05262): a focused, controlled stimulus set built to expose a mechanism, not a benchmark and not an attempt at comprehensive coverage of Indian culture. We are not trying to find everything; we build precisely what the measurements of Phases 2-5 require. The dataset is fixed at 60 cells (3 axes x 5 regions x 4 sub-concepts). The depth increase from v1.1 (50 to 100 items per cell; 3,000 to 6,000 total; renamed ICCD-3K to ICCD-6K) is authorized solely on statistical-power grounds and buys power, not coverage: at n=50 the Holm-Bonferroni-corrected per-cell detectable effect was d~0.59 (large effects only), the documented v1.1 weakness; at n=100 it is d~0.42 (medium), with per-cell power ~0.99 at the design effect d=0.5. The 3,000-item / 50-per-cell ICCD-3K configuration is retained verbatim as a documented fallback if sourcing the deeper pool proves infeasible.

The five phases are: dataset construction (Phase 1), layer-wise probing (Phase 2), sparse dictionary learning (Phase 3), causal validation through activation interventions (Phase 4), and synthesis (Phase 5). The inferential spine that runs through all five is a single pre-registered decision rule (Section 2): each of four mechanistic outcomes (A pure rewriting, B pure gating, C mixed-by-axis, D no signal) is mapped, in advance, to a measurable signature in every phase, so that the verdict is read off committed thresholds rather than assembled post hoc. The output is one paper at a mechanistic interpretability venue (NeurIPS, ICLR, or ACL), a public dataset release, and a pre-registered analysis plan filed immediately before the model-side validation step of Phase 1.

## 1. The Scientific Question

The behavioral fact is established. Aligned models behave differently from their base versions on culturally specific inputs, and the change is not uniformly positive. The mechanistic question, which is open, is what produces that change inside the network. Two opposing hypotheses sit in the current literature.

The first is **representation rewriting**. Alignment gradients propagate through the whole network during fine-tuning and modify the mid-layer subspaces where content is encoded. Under this view the model's internal representation of a cultural fact after alignment is genuinely different from before. There is empirical support that fine-tuning changes internal structure: differential analysis between pre-trained and fine-tuned models finds that middle layers undergo the largest changes during task adaptation (arXiv:2505.17073), and reinforcement-learning fine-tuning has been shown to change the intensity and distribution of internal circuit activations (arXiv:2509.21044).

The second is **late-stage policy gating**, the mechanistic form of the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis. This hypothesis, introduced with LIMA (Zhou et al., 2024, arXiv:2305.11206) and reinforced by URIAL (Lin et al., 2023, arXiv:2312.01552), holds that capabilities and knowledge are learned almost entirely in pretraining and that alignment makes superficial adjustments to how that knowledge is expressed. URIAL found that for most token positions an aligned model's top-ranked token is already within the base model's top few candidates, and that base models with a suitable prompt behave very much like aligned models. Later work formalized the notion of "superficial knowledge" and found that a large part of what alignment adds can be captured by a shallow restyling transformation (Chen et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.04602). Independent analysis of alignment as distribution shaping reached the same conclusion: aligned model behavior corresponds to a re-weighted subdistribution of base model behavior rather than new content (Lake et al., 2024, arXiv:2406.17692). Under this view the cultural knowledge is still in the network after alignment, and what changed is a late mechanism that intercepts and redirects it.

These two hypotheses can produce identical behavioral outputs, so behavior alone cannot distinguish them. They differ in where in the network the change lives and whether mid-layer content is altered, which is exactly what mechanistic interpretability tools can measure. The refusal-direction work gives the cleanest handle on why the base-vs-aligned contrast is informative: the relevant direction is already present in the base model and is merely repurposed by fine-tuning (Arditi et al., 2024, arXiv:2406.11717), which is the signature gating predicts against the rewriting alternative. A recent survey documents that mechanistic interpretability has only begun to be applied to RLHF and that the relationship between alignment and internal representations is an open research direction (Naseem, 2026, arXiv:2602.11180). This is the gap the project fills.

The third question, which we approach with more caution, is whether the effect depends on the alignment recipe and the people who produced it. There is a documented mechanism by which annotator composition could cause cultural flattening. The "Alignment Trilemma" (Sahoo et al., 2025, arXiv:2511.19504) formalizes it: when annotators from different cultural backgrounds disagree about whether a response is good, capturing both views makes the reward model noisy; the standard fix is stronger regularization, which pulls the policy toward the majority preference and erases the minority view. This is consistent with broader findings that RLHF amplifies majority viewpoints and collapses diverse preferences (Xiao et al., 2024, arXiv:2405.16455), and with the general observation that RLHF embeds the values of whoever provides the feedback. Whether this mechanism is the actual cause of the cultural effects we measure is something our design can speak to only suggestively, for reasons explained in Section 3.

Why the answer matters: if alignment installs a late-stage filter, the underlying knowledge is intact and in principle recoverable through activation-level intervention, and the cultural effect is in some sense a surface property. If alignment rewrites mid-layer representations, the knowledge is genuinely altered and cannot be recovered without retraining. The two cases call for different remediation strategies, which is why locating the mechanism is useful beyond the specific case of Indian culture.

### 1.5 Related Work and Positioning

This project sits at the intersection of four literatures. Stating each precisely, and stating exactly where this study diverges, is what lets us claim a specific contribution rather than a re-run of any one line. The unifying observation across all four is that the rewrite-vs-gate question has been *posed* in several forms but never *located* in the network for culturally specific content under a paired base-vs-aligned design.

**(1) The Superficial Alignment Hypothesis lineage.** LIMA (Zhou et al., 2024, arXiv:2305.11206) introduced the hypothesis in its modern form: a model's knowledge and capabilities are learned almost entirely in pretraining, and alignment teaches it which subdistribution of formats and styles to surface, demonstrated by competitive instruction-following from only 1,000 curated examples with no RLHF. URIAL (Lin et al., 2023, arXiv:2312.01552) sharpened it into a token-level claim: across positions, an aligned model's chosen token is usually already in the base model's top candidates, and a base model with a three-example in-context prompt approximates the aligned model's behavior, so alignment is largely a shallow re-ranking. "Extracting and Understanding the Superficial Knowledge in Alignment" (Chen et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.04602, NAACL 2025) made the claim constructive: it isolates a shallow "superficial knowledge" transformation that recovers much of the alignment delta, implying that part of alignment is a thin restyling layer rather than deep representational change. The Overton/distributional-pluralism analysis (Lake et al., 2024, arXiv:2406.17692, NAACL 2025) reached the compatible conclusion from the output-distribution side: aligned behavior is a re-weighting of a subdistribution already present in the base model rather than the injection of new content. **How we differ.** This lineage is almost entirely *behavioral and distributional*; it argues that knowledge persists but does not localize where in the residual stream the re-ranking is implemented, nor does it test culturally specific content where flattening is documented. We convert "superficial vs deep" into a *mechanistic, layer-resolved, causal* claim: gating predicts preserved mid-layer extractability with the alignment delta concentrated in late Direct Logit Attribution and in a small set of late alignment-exclusive Crosscoder features (Section 2, Outcome B), whereas rewriting predicts mid-layer extractability loss and shifted/exclusive features at the mid-layer ROME site (Outcome A). The Superficial Alignment Hypothesis is, in our framing, the prediction that Outcome B dominates.

**(2) Representation-rewriting evidence.** The opposing position has direct empirical support. Differential analysis of pre-trained vs fine-tuned models reports that middle layers undergo the largest representational change during task adaptation (arXiv:2505.17073), and reinforcement-learning fine-tuning has been shown to change the intensity and diversity of internal circuit activations (arXiv:2509.21044). **How we differ.** Both study generic task adaptation, not cultural content, and neither runs a paired causal test that could distinguish a mid-layer change that *causes* the behavioral shift from one that merely *correlates* with it. Our Phase 4 cross-checkpoint interchange patching supplies exactly that missing causal step, and our three-axis design tests whether the "middle layers change most" finding holds uniformly or only for the factual axis (Axis A) where a ROME-style mid-layer MLP association is expected.

**(3) The refusal-direction line.** Arditi et al. (2024, arXiv:2406.11717, NeurIPS 2024) showed that refusal in 13 chat models is mediated by a single difference-in-means direction `r = mu_harmful - mu_harmless` that is necessary (directional ablation removes refusal) and sufficient (activation addition induces it), and critically that the direction is *already present in the base model and merely repurposed by fine-tuning* (their Appendix J). That base-vs-aligned repurposing is the cleanest published instance of gating, and it is the template for our Axis C. The single-direction claim is contested: the Geometry of Refusal work (Wollschläger et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.17420) argues refusal occupies a multi-dimensional concept cone, and Joad et al. (2026, arXiv:2602.02132) argue there is more to refusal than one direction. **How we differ.** We do not study refusal of harmful instructions; we test whether the *same kind of late directional gate* suppresses *culturally specific recall* on sensitive topics (caste, scripture, partition, traditional medicine), with the effect sign reversed relative to the helpful axes, and we explicitly test rank >= 1 subspaces rather than assuming one dimension, taking the contestation as a design constraint rather than a settled question.

**(4) Knowledge editing and cross-lingual misalignment.** ROME (Meng et al., 2022, arXiv:2202.05262) located factual associations in mid-layer MLP modules at the last subject token and edited them with a rank-one update, establishing both the measurement point and the proof that mid-layer weights *can* store and be rewritten. CLM-Bench (Hu et al., 2026, arXiv:2601.17397) built 1,010 native bilingual CounterFact-style pairs and showed that edits installed in one language largely fail to propagate to the other, that Chinese and English edit deltas are near-orthogonal at layer 12 of 32 in Llama-3-8B, and that "generation collapse is not knowledge erasure." **How we differ.** Editing studies inject a *counterfactual* fact and trace where the edit lands; we do not edit. We ask what *alignment itself* did to representations the model already had, using the base-vs-aligned natural experiment. CLM-Bench studies the language axis of misalignment; we study the alignment-stage axis, reuse its layer-selection diagnostic as a prior for where to read, and carry its generation-collapse caution into every null.

**The composite gap.** No prior work combines (a) a *paired base-vs-post-trained* design that attributes the change to the post-training step, (b) *three orthogonal cultural axes* that can show the mechanism differs by content type, and (c) *mechanism location* via probing, dictionary learning, and causal patching rather than behavior alone. This project is defined by that combination, with Indian culture as both a controlled testbed and a genuine subject of study, and the rewrite-vs-gate verdict as the contribution.

## 2. The Hypothesis Space

We do not choose between the hypotheses before running the experiment. The pre-registered analysis plan lists four mechanistic outcomes, each with a distinct measurable signature stated in terms of the project's metric. The unifying readout is the per-item log-odds difference

```
delta-L(item) = sum_i [ log P(y_i | x_clean, y_<i) - log P(y_i | x_corrupted, y_<i) ]   (nats),
```

with the cross-model signal `delta = delta-L_base - delta-L_aligned`, and, for Axis A, the normalized logit difference `LD(r, r')` in `[0,1]` (Zhang & Nanda, 2024, arXiv:2309.16042), where `r` is the clean same-region target state and `r'` the corrupted item's different-region state. We use a log-odds throughout and never a raw probability, because probability is provably non-negative and so cannot detect components that *suppress* the correct answer once corruption drives its probability near zero (the probability patching effect is floored at `-P_*(r)`); a probability metric would therefore hide the very late-gating signal we are built to find (Zhang & Nanda, arXiv:2309.16042). The exact quantitative thresholds that separate the outcomes are defined in the Phase 1 specification (`plans/ICCD-6K-plan.md`, Section 4) and locked in the OSF pre-registration before any model is run. The corruption that defines `x_corrupted` is axis-specific Symmetric Token Replacement (Section 4 here; companion Section 7).

**Outcome A, pure rewriting.** The cultural representation in the aligned model is genuinely altered mid-network. Measurable signature, by phase:

- Phase 2: mid-layer linear probes show reduced extractability of the target in the aligned model relative to base, and MDL probing (Voita & Titov, 2020, arXiv:2003.12298) shows the fact has become less accessible (longer code length) in the aligned model.
- Phase 3: Crosscoder analysis classifies most cultural features as shifted or exclusive (large relative-decoder-norm, Delta-norm) rather than preserved.
- Phase 4: cross-checkpoint patching restores correct behavior when *mid-layer* residual activations (near the ROME last-subject-token site, AIE peak ~layer 15-18 in a 32-layer model) are patched from base into aligned, and late-layer patching does little. The normalized `LD_norm` recovers toward 1 only under mid-layer patching.

**Outcome B, pure gating.** The mid-layer representation is intact; a late mechanism redirects the output. This is the signature the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis predicts. Measurable signature, by phase:

- Phase 2: mid-layer probes show *preserved* extractability and MDL shows no mid-layer accessibility loss, yet the behavioral `delta` is non-zero. Direct Logit Attribution localizes the drop to late layers; the CLM-Bench last-subject-token layer-sweep diagnostic places maximal base-vs-aligned separation late rather than at the ~layer-12-of-32 mid site where the representation is read (arXiv:2601.17397).
- Phase 3: Crosscoder analysis finds most cultural features preserved plus a small set of alignment-exclusive features concentrated in late layers.
- Phase 4: late-layer patching (or suppressing the late refusal-style direction on Axis C) restores correct behavior; mid-layer patching does not move the output.

**Outcome C, mixed by axis.** Different cultural categories show different signatures, the outcome prior work makes most plausible because the three axes probe mechanisms the literature already treats as distinct (factual recall, lexical mapping, refusal). Measurable signature: the per-axis verdicts diverge.

- Axis A (regional facts) shows the mid-layer-MLP / last-subject-token rewriting or shifting signature of a ROME-style factual association.
- Axis B (indigenous vocabulary) shows a directional shift toward a generic decoder direction.
- Axis C (sensitive topics) shows the late-gating signature, with restoration achieved by intervening on a late refusal-style direction (Arditi et al., 2024, arXiv:2406.11717) rather than on mid-layer content, and with `delta` of the *reversed* sign predicted by the Axis C counterfactual (Section 4).

If that three-way pattern holds, the pattern itself is the contribution.

**Outcome D, no detectable signature.** None of the methods produce a clear pattern: per-cell `delta` is not distinguishable from zero at adequate power, probes and Crosscoders show no consistent classification, and patching does not reliably move behavior. The pre-registration commits us to reporting this honestly. Two cited cautions bound the interpretation of any null:

- A null patching result does not prove a representation is absent, because backup / self-repair heads can silently compensate for an ablated component (IOI, Wang et al., 2023, arXiv:2211.00593).
- Generation collapse is not knowledge erasure, so a null log-odds shift can be late gating of expression rather than absence of content (CLM-Bench, arXiv:2601.17397).

A null is therefore reported as "no detected mid-layer change," never as "no representation." A sound null on a well-motivated question with adequate power (per-cell n=100, per-axis n=2,000, per-region n=1,200) remains publishable.

### 2.1 Decision-rule table: outcome to measurable signature, by phase

The following table is the project's inferential spine. For each outcome it states the signature that must hold *jointly* across Phase 2 (descriptive), Phase 3 (structural), and Phase 4 (causal), so that the verdict is read off committed criteria, not assembled after the fact. Where the companion (`plans/ICCD-6K-plan.md`, Section 4) defines a quantitative threshold it is stated here; the remaining thresholds are flagged **[lock in pre-reg]** and will be fixed in the OSF pre-registration filed immediately before Stage 8, before any model-side number is seen. All probe and MDL comparisons are base-vs-aligned at the ROME last-subject-token read position; "mid" means the ~layer-12-to-18 band (CLM-Bench maximal-separation layer 12 of 32; ROME AIE peak layer 15, edit site layer 18, scaled to the 32-layer Llama-3-8B); "late" means roughly the final third of layers.

| Phase / metric | Outcome A: pure rewriting | Outcome B: pure gating | Outcome C: mixed by axis | Outcome D: no signature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Behavior** (`delta = delta-L_base - delta-L_aligned`, paired t-test, Holm at 60/3/5) | `delta != 0` at mid-effect power (corrected detectable d=0.42 at n=100); sign positive on A/B | `delta != 0`; same behavioral magnitude as A but produced late | `delta != 0` on A/B (positive), `delta < 0` on C (reversed sign by design) | `delta` indistinguishable from 0 at n=100, tight CI around zero |
| **P2 linear probe** (logistic regression accuracy per layer, base vs aligned) | mid-layer accuracy **drops** base->aligned (delta-accuracy below **[lock in pre-reg]**, target ~0.05-0.10 absolute) | mid-layer accuracy **preserved** (delta-accuracy within noise band **[lock in pre-reg]**) | drop on A, preserved on B/C (per-axis pattern diverges) | no consistent per-layer accuracy pattern across cells |
| **P2 MDL code length** (online coding, nats; aligned minus base) | mid-layer code length **increases** in aligned (fact less accessible; threshold **[lock in pre-reg]**) | mid-layer code length **unchanged**, despite non-zero `delta` | increase on A, unchanged on B/C | no consistent code-length change |
| **P2 layer-wise KL** (Logit-Lens KL per layer, base vs aligned) | max KL concentrated at **mid** layers (~12-18) | max KL concentrated at **late** layers | A mid, C late, B intermediate | KL flat / no clear peak |
| **P2 Direct Logit Attribution** (projection onto `r - r'` unembedding) | mid-layer MLP component carries the base-vs-aligned drop | **late** attention/MLP components carry the drop | A mid-MLP, C late-attention | no component reliably carries a drop |
| **P3 Crosscoder Delta-norm class** | most cultural features **shifted / exclusive** (Delta-norm far from 0.5; threshold **[lock in pre-reg]**, target outside [0.3, 0.7]) | most cultural features **preserved** (Delta-norm ~0.5) **plus** a small set of **late alignment-exclusive** features | shifted on A, preserved+late-exclusive on C | features neither cleanly preserved nor cleanly shifted; classification unstable across seeds |
| **P3 Skip Transcoder** (mid-layer MLP, base vs aligned) | relevant MLP feature **destroyed / replaced** | relevant MLP feature **retained**, redirected downstream | per-axis | no interpretable MLP feature isolated |
| **P4 patching restoration profile** (`LD_norm` in [0,1] by patched layer) | `LD_norm -> 1` under **mid-layer** patch; late patch does little | `LD_norm -> 1` under **late-layer** patch (or refusal-direction intervention on C); mid patch does little | restoration peak at mid for A, late for C | no patch reliably moves `LD_norm` toward 1; completeness check fails to localize |
| **P4 refusal-direction test (Axis C)** | not the operative mechanism (A/B are factual/lexical) | directional ablation of the late `r` **restores** the suppressed sensitive target; activation addition **suppresses** it (necessity + sufficiency) | operative on C only | neither ablation nor addition moves the Axis-C target |
| **Verdict mapping** | rewriting dominates on all axes | gating dominates on all axes (Superficial Alignment confirmed mechanistically) | mechanism is content-type-specific (the pattern is the result) | reported as "no detected mid-layer change," never "no representation" |

Reading the table: an outcome is supported only when its row holds across phases, and the three phases are designed to fail differently under each hypothesis, so a spurious agreement in one phase cannot by itself carry a verdict. The **[lock in pre-reg]** thresholds for probe-accuracy deltas, MDL code-length deltas, and Crosscoder Delta-norm cut-points are calibrated on the held-out set (600 items, seed 137, not analyzed in the main study) and the background-corpus Crosscoder before the confirmatory run, then frozen. Where the companion already fixes a number (the n=100 corrected detectable d=0.42, the Holm family sizes 60/3/5, the Stage-8 1.0-nat base-model floor, the `LD_norm` [0,1] normalization), that number is authoritative and is not re-derived here.

Separately from the mechanism axis (A through D), there is the **annotation-and-recipe question**: does the Indic-focused post-trained model (Sarvam-M) show a different representational signature than the human-preference-RLHF models (Llama-3-Instruct, Gemma-2-it) on the same content? We treat this as a bounded comparison whose inference is limited by confounds rather than a clean hypothesis test, because the available models vary several factors at once (Section 3). The pre-registration states the comparison we will run and explicitly states what it can and cannot establish.

## 3. The Model Suite

We study paired checkpoints. Each pair has a base model and a post-trained version derived from it, which is what lets us attribute a measured representational change to the post-training step rather than to differences in pretraining. The suite is a set of co-equal arms, each covering a fine-tuning-based alignment method; together they test the mechanism question, and one arm additionally bears on the recipe-and-annotation question with appropriate caveats. All arms are primary; the base-vs-aligned logic and statistical machinery apply equally to every one.

**Pair 1, a primary arm (human-preference RLHF): Llama-3-8B and Llama-3-8B-Instruct.** Architecturally 32 layers, hidden dimension 4096, vocabulary 128256. The aligned version uses supervised fine-tuning followed by human-preference RLHF. This pair connects directly to published work because the refusal-direction literature, including Arditi et al. (2024, arXiv:2406.11717), is benchmarked on these checkpoints, and the activation-patching tooling and best practices we adopt (Zhang & Nanda, 2024, arXiv:2309.16042) are developed on this model family, which makes our Axis C results directly comparable to published work. Concretely, Arditi et al. select the Llama-3-8B refusal direction at layer 12 of 32, position -5, which both fixes the layer band our Axis C work should center on and supplies a directly comparable published anchor. Meta-Llama-3-8B (base) is also the reference model for Phase 1's model-side validation, being the only model in the suite with no Indic-specific or preference post-training.

**Pair 2, a primary arm (human-preference RLHF), architectural robustness: Gemma-2-9B and Gemma-2-9B-it.** A different shape (42 layers, hidden dimension 3584, vocabulary 256000) aligned by a different team (Google) with supervised fine-tuning and preference-based reinforcement learning. Including this pair lets us check that any finding from Pair 1 is not an artifact of one architecture or one lab's alignment process. The 42-layer depth means the mid/late layer bands of the Section 2.1 table are rescaled proportionally (mid ~16-24, late beyond ~30) rather than copied from the 32-layer Llama numbers.

Pairs 1 and 2 are two co-equal arms covering human-preference RLHF applied to a primarily-English base. Alongside Pair 3 they answer the central question, namely what post-training alignment does to Indian cultural representations, and whether the answer is the same across architectures, labs, and fine-tuning methods.

**Pair 3, a primary arm (SFT + RLVR via GRPO): Mistral-Small-3.1-24B-Base-2503 and Sarvam-M.** Mistral-Small-3.1-24B has 40 layers and hidden dimension 5120 (verified against the published Hugging Face configuration). Sarvam-M is a post-trained derivative built by Sarvam AI, an Indian team, on the Mistral base. It is the closest available thing to a "non-Western alignment" of a known Western base, which is why it is in the suite, and its distinct fine-tuning recipe (SFT plus RLVR via GRPO) makes it a co-equal arm covering a different alignment method.

The important caveat, which corrects an earlier and too-clean version of this plan: **Sarvam-M is not produced by human-preference RLHF.** According to Sarvam's technical report (sarvam.ai/blogs/sarvam-m), Sarvam-M is built with supervised fine-tuning on a curated set of roughly 5.2 million prompts, followed by reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) using the GRPO algorithm. RLVR rewards verifiable correctness on tasks like mathematics, code, and instruction-following; it does not optimize a learned model of human preference about tone or cultural appropriateness in the way classic RLHF does. Furthermore, the supervised fine-tuning completions were generated using other approved models (which may themselves be Western-aligned) and then filtered, and the team reported explicitly adjusting outputs to reduce bias and improve cultural relevance.

The consequence for inference is that Pair 3 differs from Pairs 1 and 2 along at least three confounded dimensions simultaneously: the team and its cultural context (Indian vs Western), the training-data language mix (Indic-heavy vs English-heavy), and the alignment algorithm (SFT plus RLVR vs human-preference RLHF). Therefore a difference between Sarvam-M and the human-preference-RLHF models cannot be attributed to annotator origin alone. We state this plainly in the paper. A clean isolation of annotator origin would require holding the base model and the alignment algorithm fixed while varying only the annotator pool, and no public model pair provides that. The Sarvam-M comparison is suggestive evidence about whether Indic-focused post-training as a bundle produces a different representational signature, not a controlled manipulation of who annotated.

**TO VERIFY before Phase 4 on Pair 3.** Sarvam reported exploring a vocabulary extension with Indian-language tokens but found it degraded the knowledge base and that the distillation route with vocabulary changes did not beat plain supervised fine-tuning, which strongly suggests the shipped Sarvam-M retained Mistral's original tokenizer. Cross-checkpoint activation patching requires that base Mistral and Sarvam-M tokenize identical text into identical token sequences at identical positions; Zhang & Nanda emphasize that *which token is corrupted determines what is localized* (arXiv:2309.16042), so position correspondence is not optional. Before any patching on Pair 3, the pipeline runs the Phase 1 tokenizer-equality gate test: load both tokenizers, assert equal vocabulary size, and assert identical tokenization of every prefix and target on a battery of ICCD-6K items. If the assertion fails, Pair 3 is restricted to representation-level analyses that do not require token-position correspondence (probing and Crosscoder feature classification), and cross-checkpoint patching for Pair 3 is dropped with the reason documented. Tokenizers are pinned by commit hash for Llama-3-8B, Llama-3.1-8B, Gemma-2-9B, and Mistral-Small-3.1-24B-Base-2503.

All models are open-weight and downloadable from Hugging Face. All are loaded in bfloat16. None require special access agreements. We pin a Hugging Face commit hash for every checkpoint.

## 4. Phase 1: Dataset Construction (ICCD-6K)

This is the foundation; everything downstream depends on a clean probe set. The full specification lives in the companion document `plans/ICCD-6K-plan.md` (v1.2), which covers every variable, threshold, source, filter, and locked decision. This section is the summary needed to follow the rest of the plan and does not duplicate the companion.

**What it is.** ICCD-6K is a 6,000-item controlled minimal-pair probe set for a mechanistic-interpretability study of what post-training alignment (via any fine-tuning method: SFT, RLHF, DPO, RLVR/GRPO, ...) does to cultural knowledge inside LLMs — whether alignment rewrites mid-layer representations or gates them late, and whether the answer differs by content type. Indian culture is both the controlled testbed and a genuine subject of study. It is a purpose-built controlled probe set within a fixed and bounded scope, in the CounterFact/ROME lineage (Meng et al., NeurIPS 2022, arXiv:2202.05262) — not a benchmark and not an attempt at comprehensive coverage of Indian culture; we are not trying to find everything, we build precisely what the measurements of Phases 2-5 require.

**Structure.** Each item is a clean prefix containing one specific Indian cultural anchor, a length/suffix-matched corrupted prefix in which the anchor is replaced, and a token-level target answer (a state name or a topic name). The set is 60 cells of 100 items: 3 axes x 5 regions x 4 sub-concepts. The three axes instantiate the three mechanistic predictions of Section 2: Axis A (Regional Specificity: festivals, costume and textile, cuisine, rituals and ceremonies) probes factual recall and is expected to show shifting or rewriting at a ROME-style mid-layer site; Axis B (Cultural Flattening: classical dance, classical music, visual art, architecture and built form) probes indigenous-to-generic lexical mapping; Axis C (Sensitive Policy: social structure and caste, religion and scripture, history and political memory, traditional medicine) probes refusal and is expected to show late gating, connecting to the refusal-direction line of work. The five regions are the INDICA partition North/South/East/West/Central (Madhusudan et al., ACL 2026 Main Conference, arXiv:2601.15550). Within every cell the targets are stratified 50/30/20 by Llama-3 tokenized length (50 one-token, 30 two-token, 20 three-token; targets longer than three tokens are excluded), to keep token length from confounding any between-cell effect.

**Six anchor numbers that must not drift** (companion Section 0): total 6,000; 100 items per cell; 60 cells (breadth fixed); 50/30/20 token balance; ~6h plus ~250k Wikipedia calls construction target; Holm-Bonferroni applied at family sizes 60 (cells), 3 (axes), 5 (regions).

**Depth, not breadth (v1.2).** The only change from v1.1 is items per cell (50 to 100), authorized solely on statistical-power grounds; breadth (60 cells) is held fixed. Depth buys power, not coverage, and we make no coverage claim. The per-cell primary test is a paired two-sided t-test (power 0.80, alpha 0.05), with minimum detectable effect `d = (z_{alpha/2} + z_{beta}) / sqrt(n)`: uncorrected numerator `1.96 + 0.842 = 2.802`, Holm-Bonferroni worst case (per-comparison alpha 0.05/60) numerator ~4.18. At n=50 the Holm-corrected detectable d was 0.59 (large effects only) — the documented v1.1 weakness; at n=100 the corrected floor is d=0.42 (medium) and per-cell power at the design effect d=0.5 is ~0.99. n=128 buys only 0.42 to 0.37, not worth ~28% more sourcing, so 100 is the smallest depth that fixes the weakness. Aggregations are well-powered regardless: per-axis n=2,000 detects d~0.06, per-region n=1,200 detects d~0.08. The 3,000-item / 50-per-cell **ICCD-3K configuration is retained as a documented fallback**, accepting the large-effects-only cell sensitivity if the deeper pool cannot be sourced; the file name remains `ICCD-3K-plan.md` by instruction. A `power_table.csv` ships as an audit trail.

**Metric.** Per-item delta-L in nats (above); Axis A primary readout is the normalized logit difference `LD(r, r')` in `[0,1]` (Zhang & Nanda, arXiv:2309.16042), with `r` the clean target state and `r'` the corrupted different-region state; cross-model signal `delta = delta-L_base - delta-L_aligned`. Always a log-odds / logit, never a probability, for the non-negativity reason in Section 2.

**Counterfactual strategy (resolved; companion decision D9).** The v1.1 open flag is closed: the corruption rule is axis-specific, and all corruption is Symmetric Token Replacement (in-distribution, length/suffix-matched under the Llama-3 tokenizer), never Gaussian noising (which is out-of-distribution and can manufacture a false mid-layer "rewrite" peak two to five times the STR peak; Zhang & Nanda, arXiv:2309.16042). Sliding-window MLP patching is avoided as the primary readout because it inflates mid-layer localization (1.40x/1.75x/1.59x at windows 3/5/10); the swap point is fixed per axis and single layers are patched first.

- **Axis A, cross-anchor swap.** The corrupted prefix names a same-sub-concept cultural item whose gold region differs from the clean item's, defining `r'`. This mirrors ROME's neighborhood-specificity lesson — distractors must be same-category and close in latent space (arXiv:2202.05262). It replaces the rejected v1.1 generic swap (e.g. "Onam" to "a traditional festival"), which deleted the whole subject and left no defined `r'`, conflating loss of binding with loss of any cultural entity. Selection is deterministic under seed 271: same sub-concept, different gold region, length-matched (relaxation to +/-1 token logged as a deviation).
- **Axis B, description-based.** Indigenous-concept description to generic-Western-concept description; the target is the indigenous term (e.g. Alap, mudra, raga). Only the descriptor span is swapped; the carrier template and target are held identical. Expected direction matches A (clean > corrupted).
- **Axis C, description-based, reversed.** Culturally-specific framing to neutral/global framing; the target is the topic name (e.g. caste, jati, Vedas, Ayurveda, Partition). The expected effect direction is **reversed**: gating suppresses the sensitive answer under the culturally-specific (clean) framing, so clean may be less than corrupted and delta-L may be negative. This is the only axis where the corrupted-prefix log-odds of the target may exceed the clean-prefix log-odds; the reversed direction is stated in the OSF pre-registration before model-side validation. Generic replacements for B and C are drawn from a closed, pre-registered vocabulary table fixed under seed 271 so wording cannot be fished post hoc.

**Sourcing (companion decision D5).** Items are sourced through a hybrid pipeline:

- SANSKRITI (Maji et al., ACL Findings 2025, arXiv:2506.15355; 21,853 items) for anthropologically grounded cultural facts.
- Wikipedia for clean factual sentences and a deterministic gazetteer used to detect place-name leakage.
- A whitelisted web-search fallback (capped at <=10% of items) only for cells that would otherwise fall below the floor.

**Quality assurance (two-tier, automate-then-human; companion decision D7).** QA is two-tier and automated-first.

- Tier 1 is fully automated and reproducible: deterministic filters F1-F7 (target tokenization length, geographic leakage, linguistic leakage, corruption verification, suffix matching, length floor and ceiling; ~40% expected attrition), mandatory dual cross-validation of every item against Wikipedia AND SANSKRITI (zero uncross-validated items at release), and the Stage-8 model-side delta-L threshold.
- Tier 2 is human review restricted to the automated-passing set: spot-check inter-rater agreement requiring Cohen's kappa >= 0.6 for release, three construct-validity audits (cross-axis classification, cross-region target naming on Axis A, counterfactual-quality guessing), and an Axis C harms review by a South Asian social/religious-studies expert.

Automation is first for reproducibility, cost, and scale at 6,000 items; human review is second and mandatory because LLM judges over-merge regional consensus (INDICA recorded 28.9% / 24.5% human override rates, almost all removals; arXiv:2601.15550), so any LLM-assisted judgment must be human-audited and regional attribution is always decided by a human.

**Model-side validation (Stage 8).** A validation step on Meta-Llama-3-8B (base) — the cleanest stand-in for the pre-alignment representation, the only model in the suite with no Indic-specific or preference post-training — keeps only items whose base-model clean-vs-corrupted contrast clears an axis-specific floor. An item the base model cannot distinguish has no mid-layer representation for alignment to rewrite or gate, so it measures nothing.

- Inclusion thresholds: delta-L_base > 1.0 nat (one-sided) for Axes A and B; |delta-L_base| > 1.0 nat (two-sided) for Axis C, because Axis C's expected direction is reversed and a one-sided floor would discard exactly its signal. The 1.0-nat threshold (target ~2.7x more probable under the clean prefix) is a fixed design decision, not empirically tuned, so the ~30% attrition cannot be reverse-engineered to hit a target n.
- Selection-on-the-DV guards: the OSF pre-registration is filed immediately before Stage 8; the full per-cell base-model delta-L distribution over retained AND dropped items is published; the scientific estimand is base-minus-aligned so the symmetric base-model gate largely differences out; final composition among survivors is random within token stratum (seed 42), never ranked by effect size.
- Null caveat: a null delta-L is never read as "representation absent" — it can be gating of expression (CLM-Bench, arXiv:2601.17397) or masking by self-repair / backup heads (IOI, arXiv:2211.00593). Stage 8 filters for measurable signal; it does not adjudicate rewrite-vs-gate, which is Phases 2-5.

Phase 1 ends with a released, pre-registered, version-locked dataset ready for inference, plus the published power table and dropped-item archive.

## 5. Phase 2: Layer-Wise Probing

Before the heavier machinery, we locate where in each model the cultural information lives and whether it is accessible to downstream computation. The unit of analysis is the residual-stream activation read at the position immediately before the first target token — the ROME last-subject-token measurement point, where the average indirect effect of a factual association peaks at a mid-layer MLP (around layer 15 in GPT-2 XL; the analogous early site in a 32-layer Llama-3-8B falls in roughly the layer-15-to-18 band) (Meng et al., 2022, arXiv:2202.05262). We extract this per item, per layer, per prefix version, per model, and compute four diagnostics. Output is descriptive and feeds the Section 2.1 decision-rule table.

**Notation.** Let `h^(l)(x) in R^d` be the residual-stream activation at the read position for prompt `x` after layer `l`, with `l in {0, ..., L-1}` (`L=32` for Llama-3-8B, `42` for Gemma-2-9B). For model `M in {base, aligned}` and item `i`, write `h_M^(l)(x_i)` for the clean-prefix activation. The target label for the probe is the item's gold answer token (state name on A, indigenous term on B, topic name on C). The grid is centred on the mid band: we probe every layer but report and threshold against the mid sites (the ROME ~layer-15-to-18 early site and the CLM-Bench layer-12-of-32 maximal-separation diagnostic, arXiv:2601.17397) and the late third.

**Linear probing measures presence.** For each layer `l` and model `M` we fit a multinomial logistic-regression probe `g(h) = softmax(W h + b)` to predict the gold target token from the activation, trained on a within-cell split disjoint from the evaluation items (probe weights are fit, not the model). The diagnostic is per-layer top-1 accuracy `acc_M(l)`, and the base-vs-aligned contrast

```
delta_acc(l) = acc_base(l) - acc_aligned(l).
```

High accuracy means the layer linearly encodes the fact; low accuracy means the fact is absent or non-linearly encoded. A base-to-aligned drop in mid-layer accuracy (`delta_acc(l) > 0` at mid `l`, beyond the noise band locked in pre-registration) is a rewriting signature (Outcome A); `delta_acc(l) ~ 0` at mid `l` with a non-zero behavioral `delta` is consistent with gating (Outcome B). The probe is regularized (L2, penalty fixed on the held-out set) so probe capacity does not manufacture accuracy; we report a control probe on a label-shuffled target to bound chance accuracy per cell.

**Minimum Description Length probing measures accessibility.** Information can be present but practically unusable by the model's own downstream layers; linear accuracy alone cannot distinguish "present and accessible" from "present but buried." MDL probing via *online (prequential) coding* (Voita & Titov, 2020, arXiv:2003.12298) quantifies accessibility as the total code length needed to transmit the labels given the activations. The online code processes the `n` examples in a fixed order, training the probe on the first `t` examples and using it to encode example `t+1`, so the description length is the running sum of negative log-likelihoods:

```
L_online(y_{1:n} | h)  =  - sum_{t=1}^{n-1}  log_2 p_theta_t( y_{t+1} | h(x_{t+1}) )   (bits),
```

where `theta_t` is the probe fit on `{(h(x_1), y_1), ..., (h(x_t), y_t)}`. Equivalently the codelength decomposes as data codelength plus model codelength; a *shorter* total codelength means the labels are more readily extractable from the activations by a low-complexity probe, i.e. more *accessible*, not merely present. The per-layer accessibility contrast is

```
delta_MDL(l) = L_online^aligned(l) - L_online^base(l)   (bits; report also compression ratio
               = uniform-code length / L_online, larger = more accessible).
```

A mid-layer MDL *increase* in the aligned model (`delta_MDL(l) > 0` at mid `l`, threshold locked in pre-registration) means alignment made the cultural fact harder to extract — a rewriting signature (Outcome A). Unchanged MDL at mid `l` despite a non-zero behavioral `delta` means the fact is still as accessible mid-network and the behavioral change must come from elsewhere — a gating signature (Outcome B). MDL is the cleaner of the two presence/accessibility tests precisely because online codelength penalizes a probe that only fits with high capacity, so it does not reward the "buried but linearly decodable with a large probe" case that plain accuracy can.

**Layer-wise KL divergence locates divergence.** Running both checkpoints in a pair and comparing their predicted next-token distributions at each layer through the Logit Lens (decoding the intermediate residual stream directly through the unembedding) shows the layer at which the two models most diverge, pointing to where the alignment effect is concentrated. Define the per-layer Logit-Lens distribution `P_M^(l) = softmax(W_U * LN(h_M^(l)))` and the per-layer cross-model divergence

```
KL(l) = D_KL( P_base^(l)(. | x_clean)  ||  P_aligned^(l)(. | x_clean) ),
```

averaged over the cell. The layer `l* = argmax_l KL(l)` localizes the alignment effect: `l*` in the mid band is a rewriting signature, `l*` in the late third is a gating signature. The CLM-Bench layer-selection diagnostic, which sweeps the last-subject-token layer and reports maximal base-vs-edited separation around layer 12 of 32 in Llama-3-8B (arXiv:2601.17397), gives a concrete prior for where the cultural read happens and a check that we are reading the representation at the right depth. KL is a legitimate patching metric and a sound divergence measure (Zhang & Nanda, arXiv:2309.16042), but unlike `delta-L` it is non-negative and unsigned, so it locates *where* the models differ without saying whether the aligned model suppresses or promotes the target; we therefore read KL alongside the signed DLA below, never alone.

**Direct Logit Attribution locates the decision.** Projecting each layer's attention and MLP outputs onto the target-minus-alternative unembedding direction gives a per-component contribution to the answer. For Axis A the direction is the unembedding of the clean state `r` minus the corrupted state `r'`; for B/C it is the gold target minus a matched alternative. With component output `c^(l)` (an attention-head output or an MLP-block output written to the read position) and unembedding rows `W_U[r], W_U[r']`, the direct contribution is

```
DLA(c^(l)) = LN(c^(l)) . ( W_U[r] - W_U[r'] )   (nats of logit difference),
```

and the base-vs-aligned drop attributed to a component is `DLA_base(c^(l)) - DLA_aligned(c^(l))`. This is the logit-difference attribution used throughout the activation-patching literature (Zhang & Nanda, arXiv:2309.16042; the IOI logit-difference metric `logit(IO) - logit(S)`, Wang et al., arXiv:2211.00593). A mid-layer MLP component carrying the drop is a rewriting signature; a late attention/MLP component carrying it (the structure Arditi et al. find for refusal, where late name-mover-like heads write the decision, arXiv:2406.11717) is a gating signature. DLA catches a late-stage filter that mid-layer probes would miss, which is why it pairs with the probe/MDL accessibility tests rather than replacing them.

Read together, the four diagnostics produce a per-axis, per-layer picture mapped directly onto the Section 2.1 table: preserved linear accuracy and MDL with a late DLA drop and a late KL peak is the Outcome B gating signature; a mid-layer MDL increase with a reduced mid-layer probe, a mid KL peak, and a mid-MLP DLA drop is the Outcome A rewriting signature; per-axis divergence is Outcome C. Phase 2 output is descriptive and motivates the causal work in Phase 4. We follow the IOI guidance to average logit-difference readouts over large item counts (N>200-scale) for stability; our per-cell n=100 and the 1,200-2,000-item aggregations clear this comfortably (Wang et al., arXiv:2211.00593).

## 6. Phase 3: Dictionary Learning

Probing says where information lives, not which features encode it. To reach feature level we decompose the residual stream into a sparse dictionary, using two complementary tools. A standing design decision, inherited from ROME's covariance pre-caching practice (ROME estimates its key covariance `C` from ~100,000 background Wikipedia keys, never from the editing items; arXiv:2202.05262), is that the dictionaries are trained on a large background corpus and only *applied* to ICCD-6K, never trained on it.

**BatchTopK Crosscoders for base-versus-aligned diffing.** A Crosscoder (Lindsey et al., 2024, Anthropic) is a sparse autoencoder trained jointly on two models' residual streams, learning a shared feature dictionary in which each latent feature carries one decoder vector per model. Concretely, for a feature `j` the encoder produces an activation `f_j` from the concatenated (base, aligned) residual streams, and the feature is decoded back into each model separately via decoder vectors `d_j^base` and `d_j^aligned`. The **relative decoder norm**, the Delta-norm statistic, is

```
Delta-norm(j) = || d_j^aligned ||  /  ( || d_j^base || + || d_j^aligned || )   in [0, 1],
```

so a feature whose two decoder vectors have similar norm sits near 0.5 (**preserved**), a feature whose aligned decoder norm is near zero sits near 0 and a feature whose base decoder norm is near zero sits near 1 (the two **exclusive** classes), and intermediate imbalances are **shifted**. The classification rule (cut-points locked in pre-registration, target preserved-band roughly [0.4, 0.6]) is the structural test that feeds the Section 2.1 table: a preponderance of cultural features classified shifted/exclusive supports rewriting (Outcome A), while cultural features classified preserved plus a small set of *aligned-exclusive* features concentrated in late layers supports gating (Outcome B). We use the **BatchTopK** sparsity formulation (Bussmann, Leask & Nanda, 2024, arXiv:2412.06410), which selects, across a whole training batch, the top-K feature activations rather than enforcing a per-example L1 penalty; the chat-tuning Crosscoder work of Minder et al. (2025, arXiv:2504.02922) found that BatchTopK gives cleaner attribution than an L1 penalty and is robust to the spurious "complete-shrinkage" features that L1 Crosscoders introduce (artifact latents that look exclusive only because L1 shrinks one decoder norm). We train multiple seeds and require that a feature's preserved/shifted/exclusive label agree across seeds before it counts toward the verdict.

**Why the ~500M-token background corpus and not the 6,000-item probe set.** We train one Crosscoder per model pair on roughly 500 million tokens of background text — **FineWeb-Edu** for broad English plus **IndicCorp v2** for Indic-language and India-topic coverage — kept entirely separate from ICCD-6K. The reason is a capacity argument: a sparse dictionary that resolves cultural content needs to see a broad distribution of text to learn monosemantic features, and 6,000 short minimal-pair items (each only a prefix plus a 1-3 token target) carry far too few tokens and far too narrow a distribution to fit a large dictionary without overfitting to the probe templates. Training on the probe set would also be circular: features fit on the very items we then classify would be guaranteed to fire on them, telling us nothing about whether the feature is a genuine model component. So the dictionary is trained on background text, frozen, and then the ICCD-6K items are projected through the frozen dictionary to identify and classify the cultural features that fire on them. This is the dictionary-learning analogue of ROME's rule that the background covariance is estimated off-task; the probe set is for projection and classification, not for training.

**Skip Transcoders for the MLP function.** A Skip Transcoder (Dunefsky, Chlenski & Nanda, 2024, arXiv:2406.11944) is a sparse dictionary that predicts an MLP block's *output* from its *input* through a sparse bottleneck plus a linear skip connection, `MLP_out ~= W_skip * MLP_in + Decoder(TopK(Encoder(MLP_in)))`. Unlike a Crosscoder, which reads what is *written to the residual stream*, a transcoder exposes what the MLP block *computes* as an input-output function, with the linear skip path carrying the dense component so the sparse features capture the interpretable structure. We train Skip Transcoders on the MLP blocks that Phase 2's DLA and KL diagnostics flag as important — in particular the mid-layer MLP at the ROME last-subject-token early site for Axis A — and compare base versus aligned. If the relevant MLP feature is destroyed or replaced base->aligned, that is the rewriting signature; if the feature is retained but its output is redirected downstream, that is the gating signature. The transcoder is the feature-level instrument that distinguishes "the MLP no longer computes the cultural association" from "the MLP still computes it but a later component overrides the decision."

Phase 3 output per model pair: a frozen Crosscoder dictionary trained on the ~500M-token background corpus, with each cultural feature classified as preserved, shifted, or exclusive by Delta-norm (agreement required across training seeds), and Skip Transcoders on the MLP blocks most relevant to cultural processing. These structural classifications populate the Phase 3 rows of the Section 2.1 table and supply the specific features that Phase 4 will steer to establish causal roles.

## 7. Phase 4: Causal Validation

Probing and dictionary learning are correlational. Phase 4 tests causation through activation interventions, using the activation-patching best practices established by Zhang & Nanda (arXiv:2309.16042): Symmetric Token Replacement corruption (never Gaussian noising), logit difference rather than probability as the readout, and single-layer patching before any windowed variant.

**Cross-checkpoint interchange patching** locates the layer responsible. We patch the base model's residual stream at a chosen layer `l` and token position `p` into the aligned model's forward pass — copying `h_base^(l)(x)` over `h_aligned^(l)(x)` at position `p` and letting the rest of the aligned forward pass recompute — and measure how far the aligned output moves toward the base (clean) output. The readout is the normalized restoration metric

```
LD_norm(l) = ( LD_patched(l)(r, r') - LD_aligned(r, r') )  /  ( LD_base(r, r') - LD_aligned(r, r') )   in [0, 1],
```

where `LD(r, r') = Logit(r) - Logit(r')`, `LD_aligned` is the un-patched aligned (corrupted-equivalent) run, `LD_base` is the base (clean) run, and `LD_patched(l)` is the aligned run with layer `l` patched from base. `LD_norm = 1` means patching that layer fully restores base behavior, `LD_norm = 0` means it does nothing. Sweeping `l` produces a restoration profile: a peak at the mid band (near the layer-15-to-18 last-subject-token site) is the rewriting signature — the base model's mid-layer content is what the aligned model is missing — while a peak in the late third is the gating signature — only the base model's late computation restores behavior. This is the *interchange*/denoising direction (corrupt -> clean overwrite) the literature recommends; it requires token-position correspondence between the two checkpoints, which is guaranteed for Pairs 1 and 2 and must be verified for Pair 3 by the tokenizer-equality gate test (Section 3).

**Path and edge patching** narrow localization to specific attention heads and MLP edges. This is the technique behind the Indirect Object Identification circuit (Wang et al., 2023, arXiv:2211.00593), which reverse-engineered a 26-head, 7-class circuit using path patching and the logit-difference metric `logit(IO) - logit(S)`. Path patching isolates the *direct* effect of a sender component on a receiver set by freezing all other components to their original-run activations and patching only the sender, so paths that route through intermediate attention heads are removed; averaged over N>200 pairs. We carry forward the IOI caveat directly: because backup / self-repair heads activate only when primary heads are ablated, a null patching result does not prove a representation is absent, so we test the three IOI validation criteria rather than reading a single null as evidence of nothing there — **faithfulness** (the located component set recovers most of the base-vs-aligned effect), **completeness** (knocking out any subset of the located set degrades the aligned model the same way it degrades the located circuit, the test IOI's own circuit failed under greedy search), and **minimality** (every located component is non-redundant). This completeness discipline is what turns a "we found a layer" claim into a defensible mechanism.

**Latent feature steering** tests specific Crosscoder features. Amplifying a *shifted* feature or suppressing an *exclusive* feature (from the Phase 3 frozen dictionary) in the aligned model's forward pass, and checking whether correct cultural behavior is restored (`LD_norm` recovers toward 1), establishes the causal role of that feature beyond its Delta-norm classification. A feature that is classified shifted by Delta-norm but whose amplification does not move behavior is a correlational artifact, not a causal carrier; steering is what separates the two.

For Axis C specifically, this connects to the refusal-direction literature. Arditi et al. (2024, arXiv:2406.11717) extracted a difference-in-means refusal direction `r = mu_harmful - mu_harmless` and showed it mediates refusal across many models, with the direction already present in the base model and repurposed by fine-tuning. We test whether the same *kind* of late direction mediates suppression of culturally sensitive content, running both halves of their necessity/sufficiency pair:

- **Necessity (directional ablation).** Erase the candidate direction `r-hat` from every layer and position in the aligned model, `x' <- x - r-hat (r-hat^T x)`, and test whether the suppressed Axis-C target's log-odds is restored. If ablating the direction un-suppresses the sensitive target, the direction is necessary for the gating.
- **Sufficiency (activation addition).** Add the direction back, `x^(l)' <- x^(l) + r^(l)`, to an Axis-C item the aligned model does *not* suppress, and test whether the target is now suppressed. If so, the direction is sufficient to install the gate.

Because the single-direction account is contested (Geometry of Refusal, Wollschläger et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.17420; Joad et al., 2026, arXiv:2602.02132), our Axis C analysis tests **rank >= 1 subspaces**: we extract not just the top difference-in-means direction but an orthogonalized set, ablate the rank-1, rank-2, ... subspaces in turn, and report whether a single direction suffices for cultural refusal or whether multiple directions are needed. Throughout we keep the metric a log-odds (the refusal metric of Arditi et al. is itself a log-odds, `log P - log(1-P)`), so that suppression of the sensitive target — the reversed-sign Axis C effect — remains detectable; a probability metric would floor out exactly this suppressing component (the non-negativity argument, Section 2).

**Token-position correspondence and the Pair-3 gate.** Every patching operation above copies an activation from one checkpoint into another at a *specific token position*, and Zhang & Nanda show that *which token is corrupted (or patched) determines what is localized* (arXiv:2309.16042), so position correspondence is a precondition, not a convenience. For Pairs 1 and 2 the base and aligned models share a tokenizer, so positions correspond by construction. For Pair 3 (Mistral base vs Sarvam-M) this must be established empirically: before any cross-checkpoint patching, the pipeline runs the **Pair-3 tokenizer-equality gate** — assert equal vocabulary size and assert that both tokenizers map every ICCD-6K prefix and target to identical token sequences at identical positions on a battery of items. If the gate passes, Pair 3 enters the full patching protocol; if it fails, Pair 3 is restricted to the position-free analyses (probing and Crosscoder feature classification) and its cross-checkpoint patching is dropped with the reason documented. This gate is the hard dependency that keeps the Pair-3 contrast from producing an artifact of misaligned positions.

Phase 4 output: a per-axis, per-pair causal map of which intervention (mid-layer patch, late-layer patch, refusal-direction ablation/addition, or latent-feature steering) reliably moves aligned behavior toward base behavior, with the IOI faithfulness/completeness/minimality checks attached to every localization and every null.

## 8. Phase 5: Synthesis

Phase 5 combines the descriptive (Phase 2), structural (Phase 3), and causal (Phase 4) evidence into the paper's claim. The combination is not a judgment call; it is the pre-registered decision rule of Section 2.1, applied per axis and then aggregated.

**The per-axis combination rule (pre-registered).** For each axis (A, B, C) and each model pair, we assemble the three phases' readouts and score them against the Section 2.1 rows. An axis is assigned **rewriting (A-type)** only if it shows the rewriting signature *jointly* across phases: a mid-layer probe-accuracy drop and a mid-layer MDL increase (Phase 2, beyond the locked thresholds), a preponderance of shifted/exclusive cultural features and a destroyed/replaced mid-layer MLP feature (Phase 3), and a mid-layer-peaked `LD_norm` restoration profile (Phase 4). An axis is assigned **gating (B-type)** only if it shows, jointly: preserved mid-layer probe accuracy and unchanged mid-layer MDL despite a non-zero behavioral `delta`, a late KL peak and late DLA drop (Phase 2), preserved features plus late alignment-exclusive features (Phase 3), and a late-peaked `LD_norm` (plus, on Axis C, a refusal-direction necessity-and-sufficiency result) in Phase 4. The behavioral precondition for either assignment is a `delta` distinguishable from zero at the n=100 corrected power (detectable d=0.42), Holm-corrected within the 60/3/5 families; on Axis C `delta` is expected negative (reversed sign) and is tested two-sided so the design cannot smuggle in its expected sign. If the phases *disagree within an axis* (for example a mid MDL increase but a late `LD_norm` peak), that axis is reported as **mixed/unresolved** with the conflicting readouts shown, not forced to a verdict. If no axis clears the behavioral precondition, the result is **Outcome D**, reported as "no detected mid-layer change," never "no representation," under the self-repair and generation-collapse cautions (arXiv:2211.00593; arXiv:2601.17397).

**Aggregating to the project verdict.** With per-axis verdicts in hand, the project-level outcome is mechanical: all-rewriting across axes is Outcome A, all-gating is Outcome B, divergent per-axis verdicts (the literature's most plausible case, since the axes probe factual recall, lexical mapping, and refusal — mechanisms treated as distinct) is Outcome C, and no signal anywhere is Outcome D. Outcome C is not a failure to decide; if the three-way pattern holds (Axis A rewriting/shifting at the ROME mid site, Axis B a directional shift toward a generic decoder direction, Axis C late refusal-style gating with the reversed sign), the pattern itself is the contribution.

**Cross-pair agreement rule (Llama-3 and Gemma-2).** The two human-preference-RLHF pairs are combined into a single generality claim by a pre-registered agreement rule applied after the per-axis verdicts above are computed independently for each pair. For each axis the two pairs each yield one of {rewriting, gating, mixed/unresolved, no-signal}, and the cross-pair status is: **replicated** when both pairs return the same non-null verdict; **architecture-dependent** when both return a non-null verdict but they differ (for example Llama-3 rewriting on Axis A but Gemma-2 gating), in which case the divergence itself is reported as a finding about layer-depth or lab-recipe sensitivity rather than averaged away; and **single-pair** when exactly one pair clears the behavioral precondition (`delta` distinguishable from zero at the corrected power), reported as suggestive and explicitly not a generality claim. Because the layer bands differ by depth (Section 2.1: Llama-3 mid 12-18 of 32; Gemma-2 mid 16-24 of 42), "same verdict" is judged on the *proportional* band (mid vs late third), never on the absolute layer index, so a genuine mid-layer agreement is not mistaken for disagreement merely because the two architectures number their layers differently. The agreement rule is filed in the OSF pre-registration so that a split outcome cannot be quietly resolved in favor of whichever pair gives the cleaner story.

**The Sarvam-M rewrite-vs-gate verdict and the bounded annotation-origin read-out.** Pair 3 yields its own per-axis rewrite-vs-gate verdict by exactly the combination rule above, as a co-equal arm of the design. Separately, the *annotation-origin* question it bears on — whether Indic-focused post-training leaves a different representational signature than human-preference RLHF — is held to a bounded inferential standard, fixed in advance so the multi-factor confound of Section 3 (team, data-language mix, SFT-plus-RLVR-vs-human-preference-RLHF) cannot be laundered into a clean annotation-origin claim. Three guards apply to that annotation-origin read-out. First, Pair 3 enters the *representation-level* comparison (Phase 2 probe and MDL accessibility, Phase 3 Crosscoder Delta-norm classification) unconditionally, because those analyses need only matched read positions, not token-position correspondence; it enters the *causal* comparison (Phase 4 cross-checkpoint patching) only if the Section 3 tokenizer-equality gate passes, and if the gate fails the Phase 4 row for Pair 3 is reported as "not evaluable," never as a null. Second, any Sarvam-M-vs-RLHF difference is described as a difference between *bundles* ("Indic-focused post-training of a Western base" vs "human-preference RLHF of comparable bases"), with the three confounded dimensions named in the same sentence, and is never attributed to annotator origin alone. Third, on the annotation-origin question specifically the comparison can *raise* a hypothesis (for example that Indic-focused post-training preserves an Axis-B indigenous-term feature that human-preference RLHF shifts) but cannot *confirm* one; a Sarvam-M annotation-origin pattern that survives the held-out 600-item set is flagged as a lead for a future controlled study. The per-arm rewrite-vs-gate verdicts, by contrast, draw on all three pairs equally.

**Three reporting layers.** The paper presents the verdict in three layers. First, the per-axis rewrite-vs-gate verdict scored against the Section 2.1 signatures, including whether Axis C's reversed-sign prediction held. Second, a cross-model comparison: whether the mechanism replicates across the two human-preference-RLHF pairs (Llama-3, Gemma-2 — architectural and lab generality) and the SFT+RLVR arm (Sarvam-M — method generality), with the Sarvam-M annotation-origin read-out reported under the multi-factor caveat from Section 3 and bounded by the Pair-3 tokenizer-gate outcome (representation-level only if the gate failed). Third, a feature-level catalog of the Crosscoder features causally tied to each behavior (those that passed Phase 4 steering), named so that future interpretability work can reuse them.

**Why pre-registration makes this binding.** The analysis plan, filed on the Open Science Framework immediately before model-side validation in Phase 1, locks the statistical tests, the multiple-comparisons correction (Holm-Bonferroni at family sizes 60/3/5), the cell definitions, the four counterfactual constructions, the Section 2.1 thresholds (the **[lock in pre-reg]** entries calibrated on the held-out set and the background-corpus Crosscoder), and the per-axis combination rule above. Findings are reported as pre-registered or exploratory according to whether they map to a locked test. This addresses the standard concern that mechanistic-interpretability results are post hoc — that a researcher with many layers, many components, and many metrics can find *some* pattern — and it is what lets the fixed 60-cell scope and the fixed decision rule function as genuine commitments rather than descriptions we could later widen. The held-out 600-item set (seed 137, untouched in the main study) is reserved so that any analysis suggested by Phase 2-4 findings can be confirmed out-of-sample, preserving the integrity of the confirmatory verdict.

## 9. Cross-Cutting Concerns

**Reproducibility.** Pinned random seeds (main sampling 42, held-out 137, spot-check 314, counterfactual audit 271), pinned Hugging Face commit hashes for all checkpoints and tokenizers, a timestamped and archived Wikipedia snapshot, the published power table and dropped-item archive, and code released under MIT at publication. The pipeline is designed to re-run end-to-end on a single workstation with one A100-class GPU in roughly one week of wall-clock time. (This estimate is provisional and will be revised after the pilot, and now reflects the 2x larger candidate pool.)

**Pre-registration.** The analysis plan is filed on the Open Science Framework immediately before model-side validation (Stage 8) in Phase 1, which is the point where the inferential design begins and the construction-to-inference transition occurs. It is timestamped, citable, and is the only immutable artifact in the project.

**Compute budget.** Provisional estimate around 250-300 GPU-hours on A100-class hardware. The estimate is now broken out per phase so the dominant cost is explicit and the depth increase to 6,000 items can be traced to where it lands. These numbers are estimates and will be updated as phases execute.

| Phase | Resource | Provisional cost | Where the cost lives | Effect of 6K depth |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 (construction) | CPU + Wikipedia API; **not GPU-bound** | ~6 h wall-clock + ~250k Wikipedia calls; Stage-8 model-side validation is the only GPU touch (~8,580 candidate items x 2 prefixes, base-model forward passes, minutes-to-hours on one 8B GPU) | API throughput (category walk + summary fetch), parallelized over polite workers; Stage 8 caches clean-run activations | doubles Stage-8 forward passes and roughly doubles the candidate-sourcing API volume; no Crosscoder cost |
| Phase 2 (probing) | GPU | **largest activation-extraction share, ~80-110 GPU-h** | one forward pass per item per prefix per layer per model (6,000 items x 2 prefixes x up to 4 models, activations cached at every layer), then cheap CPU-side probe/MDL/KL/DLA fits | scales **linearly** with item count: this is where the 50->100 depth increase is paid |
| Phase 3 (dictionary learning) | GPU | **dominant single line, ~110-140 GPU-h** | training one BatchTopK Crosscoder per model pair on ~500M background tokens (FineWeb-Edu + IndicCorp v2), multiple seeds, plus Skip Transcoders on flagged MLP blocks | **independent of the 6K depth** — trained on background text, not the probe set; the depth increase does not touch this cost |
| Phase 4 (causal validation) | GPU | ~40-60 GPU-h | cross-checkpoint interchange patching (layer sweep), path/edge patching averaged over N>200 pairs, refusal-direction ablation/addition, latent-feature steering, completeness checks | scales with item count but only the surviving 6,000 enter patching; sub-dominant to Phases 2-3 |
| Phase 5 (synthesis) | CPU | negligible GPU | scoring readouts against the Section 2.1 table; held-out confirmation re-runs reuse cached activations | none |

The two GPU-dominant phases are therefore Phase 2 probing (linear in item count, so the depth increase lands here) and Phase 3 Crosscoder training (fixed by the background corpus, so the depth increase does *not* land here). The headline consequence: doubling the dataset from 3,000 to 6,000 raises Phase 2 extraction and Phase 4 patching roughly in proportion but leaves the single largest line, Crosscoder training, unchanged, which is why the total stays in the ~250-300 GPU-hour band rather than doubling. Phase 1 is CPU/API-bound and is wall-clock-limited by the Wikipedia API rather than by GPU.

## 10. Risks Across the Whole Project

**Outcome D, no signal.** Mitigation: the pre-registration commits us to publishing a sound null result, with per-cell power ~0.99 at the design effect so that a null is informative; nulls are reported as "no detected mid-layer change," never "no representation," per the self-repair and generation-collapse cautions.

**Crosscoder training instability.** Sparse dictionaries can collapse or fail to learn monosemantic features. Mitigation: BatchTopK is more stable than L1 and avoids L1's complete-shrinkage artifacts (Minder et al., arXiv:2504.02922); we train multiple seeds and require that feature classifications (preserved/shifted/exclusive) agree across seeds before a feature counts toward the verdict.

**Bare-prefix-on-aligned-model artifacts.** Feeding bare prefixes to aligned models is necessary for token-position-aligned patching (chat-template special tokens break the position correspondence that patching requires; Zhang & Nanda, arXiv:2309.16042) but is off-distribution for the aligned model. Mitigation: RLHF weight changes apply regardless of input format, bare-prefix probing is field-standard (Arditi et al., arXiv:2406.11717), and an appendix chat-template robustness check is run; if conclusions differ between bare and templated conditions, both are reported.

**Pair 3 annotation-origin confound.** Sarvam-M differs from the human-preference-RLHF models in team, data mix, and algorithm (SFT plus RLVR, not human-preference RLHF), so it cannot isolate annotator origin. This bounds the *annotation-origin* inference only; Pair 3's base-vs-aligned rewrite-vs-gate verdict is co-equal with the other arms. Mitigation: we state the annotation-origin confound in the abstract and limitations, and do not claim a clean annotation-origin result.

**Pair 3 tokenizer mismatch.** If the shipped Sarvam-M did not retain Mistral's tokenizer, cross-checkpoint patching for Pair 3 is impossible. Mitigation: the Phase 1 tokenizer-equality gate test runs before patching (Sections 3 and 7); if it fails, Pair 3 is restricted to analyses that do not need token-position correspondence.

**Sarvam-M is not from-scratch Indian.** It is a post-training of a Western base, and no from-scratch Indian base-and-aligned pair exists at scale. Mitigation: we never claim otherwise; the comparison is "Indic-focused post-training of a Western base" versus "Western post-training of comparable bases."

**Selection on the dependent variable (Stage 8).** Keeping items where the base model already shows an anchor effect can inflate effects on the surviving sample. Mitigation: OSF pre-registration filed before Stage 8, the full retained-and-dropped base-model delta-L distribution published, a fixed (not tuned) 1.0-nat threshold, random-within-stratum final composition, and the base-minus-aligned estimand that differences the symmetric gate out.

**The cultural framing itself.** We are studying Indian cultural content and are not ourselves the cultural authority. Mitigation: every item is grounded in three sources (SANSKRITI, INDICA's regional consensus, Wikipedia) with mandatory dual cross-validation, a South Asian social/religious-studies expert audits the Axis C items before release, and the dataset is released publicly with a sensitive-content dataset card for community correction.

**Refusal-direction single-direction assumption.** The single-direction claim is contested (arXiv:2502.17420, arXiv:2602.02132). Mitigation: Axis C tests rank >= 1 subspaces and reports whether one direction suffices for cultural refusal or whether multiple are needed, keeping the metric a log-odds so the reversed-sign suppression remains visible.

**Probe-capacity and MDL-comparability risk (Phase 2).** A high-capacity linear probe can read a fact that the model itself cannot use, inflating apparent presence. Mitigation: MDL online coding penalizes probe complexity, label-shuffled control probes bound chance accuracy per cell, and the probe regularization and MDL example-ordering are fixed on the held-out set before the confirmatory run so they are not tuned to the result.

## 11. What Success Looks Like

Success is three deliverables.

First, a mechanistic answer, with causal evidence, to whether post-training alignment rewrites or gates Indian cultural representations, resolved per axis against the concrete Outcome A-D signatures of Section 2.1. Any of outcomes A through D is publishable.

Second, a feature-level catalog of the Crosscoder features that encode preserved, shifted, and alignment-exclusive cultural content, with named features (those that passed Phase 4 steering) that future interpretability work can reuse.

Third, a carefully bounded comparison of whether Indic-focused post-training (Sarvam-M) leaves a different representational signature than human-preference RLHF on the same content, with the multi-factor confound stated explicitly so the annotation-origin reading is taken as suggestive rather than dispositive.

The contribution sits in mechanistic interpretability, with Indian culture as both a controlled testbed and a genuine subject of study. Primary venues: NeurIPS, ICLR, ACL.

## 12. Known Limitations

We commit to reporting these in the paper. The detailed treatment, including mitigations and residual risk per item, is in the companion Phase 1 spec, Section 11.

**English-only.** We test cultural knowledge in English prompts, not in Hindi, Tamil, or other Indian languages. English-Wikipedia editor bias over-represents elite-English-editing states (Kerala, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, West Bengal) and under-covers Bihar, Jharkhand, and the Northeast; this is shared with SANSKRITI and INDICA and is a constraint of working through model tokenizers that are primarily English. Mitigated by SANSKRITI cross-validation and the equal-n quota; residual coverage threat not eliminated.

**Five-region granularity.** South India alone contains Telugu, Tamil, Malayalam, and Kannada cultures with different practices; the five-region analysis cannot see intra-regional variation. We use five regions because INDICA validates them empirically (only 39.4% universal cross-region agreement) and because they give enough items per region (n=1,200) for statistical power. Finer granularity is explicit future scope.

**Recipe coverage.** Our arms cover human-preference RLHF (Llama, Gemma) and SFT+RLVR via GRPO (Sarvam-M) as co-equal cases. We do not separately study DPO, RLAIF, or constitutional methods, or RLVR fully in isolation; Sarvam-M bundles SFT and RLVR. Disentangling alignment recipes further is future work.

**Model coverage.** Qwen, DeepSeek, Phi, and others may differ. The current suite is what is tractable on a single-workstation budget with available base-and-aligned pairs.

**Post-training as one event.** We compare base to one post-trained snapshot; we do not study mid-training, continual pretraining, or chained alignment stages.

**Cultural Flattening dichotomy.** Axis B assumes a clean indigenous-versus-generic contrast, which is clean for some concepts (Raga versus scale) and fuzzy for others. We restrict Axis B to concepts with academic documentation of the contrast and accept the coverage cost; borderline cases are filtered and the axis is audited in Tier 2.

**Tokenization confound.** Festival names are shorter than dance-form names, and delta-L sums one term per target token, so token count is entangled with the metric. Mitigated by the 50/30/20 within-cell quota, within-bin analyses, and the >3-token exclusion; perfect decorrelation is impossible because some concept classes have no short native terms.

**Training-frequency confound.** Anchors differ by orders of magnitude in pretraining frequency (Diwali vs Bathukamma), and rarer anchors carry lower log-probs. Mitigated by shipping a per-item frequency estimate and a pre-registered frequency-control robustness check, kept out of the primary model (multiple-comparisons cost across 60 cells) by design.

**Selection-on-the-dependent-variable in model-side validation.** Phase 1's validation step keeps items where the base model already shows an effect, which can inflate measured effects on the surviving sample. Mitigation: OSF pre-registration before Stage 8 and publication of the full base-model effect-size distribution over retained and dropped items so readers can judge whether survivors are atypical; effects are reported as conditional on detectability.

**Self-repair (new in v1.2).** A null patching result does not prove that no representation exists. Backup and self-repair heads activate only when primary heads are ablated and can silently compensate downstream (IOI, Wang et al., 2023, arXiv:2211.00593; 26 heads, 7 classes, metric logit(IO) minus logit(S)). Mitigation: we never read a null as absence, we test completeness and minimality rather than faithfulness alone, we average over the N>200-scale the IOI work recommends (our aggregations clear this), and we use logit difference not probability. Full completeness verification is a Phase 4 obligation.

**Generation collapse is not knowledge erasure (new in v1.2).** An edited or aligned model can retain a fact yet fail to express it, so a null log-odds shift after alignment may be gating of expression rather than absence — exactly the gate-vs-rewrite ambiguity (CLM-Bench, Hu et al., 2026, arXiv:2601.17397; 1,010 native bilingual CounterFact-style pairs over 24 domains). Mitigation: we report both behavioral (delta-L) and representational read-outs and use the last-subject-token layer-sweep diagnostic (max separation around layer 12 of 32 in Llama-3-8B); disambiguating gate from absence is the core scientific question.

**LLM judges over-merge regional consensus (new in v1.2).** In INDICA, human reviewers overrode the LLM judge on 28.9% of inter-regional and 24.5% of universal cases (almost all removals), and 89% of model responses over-explained (arXiv:2601.15550). Mitigation: the automate-then-human QA design makes Tier-2 human review mandatory (kappa >= 0.6, regional attribution human-decided) and scores a fixed target token rather than open generation; human review is sampled, not exhaustive, at 6,000 items.

## 13. Working Title and Framing Note

The current working title is "The Selectivity of Post-Training Alignment: Mechanistic Sources of Cultural Flattening in Aligned LLMs." It leads with the alignment mechanism, names selectivity (the observation that motivates the work), and names cultural flattening (the phenomenon explained), with Indian culture as both a controlled testbed and a genuine subject of study. This positions the work for a mechanistic interpretability audience.

The title is not final and is expected to change once results are in. If the evidence favors one mechanism cleanly, the title may assert it, for example "Cultural Flattening Lives in Late Layers" if gating dominates, or "Alignment Rewrites Cultural Representations in Mid Layers" if rewriting dominates. An earlier working title ("Suppression vs. Shifting") is retired in favor of the alignment-mechanism-first framing, but the suppression-versus-shifting language may still appear in the body as a description of the two hypotheses.
